Overview

For the past year UBC Rocket has been an integral part of my life. After being inspired by Elon Musk's and SpaceX's vision and realizing that UBC had no rocketry team, I decided to start a team at UBC. The team quickly grew to 65 students. Over the span of the year, I spent increasingly more time on rocket related work. In the months leading up to competition, every night and all weekends were dedicated to making our first competition entry a success.

I was both acting as the co-captain of the team and the project manager for our first rocket, Cypress. This means that I was both responsible for overall team direction, adminstrative work, as well as being heavily involved in all technical areas of the rocket.

On the technical side, my primary responsibility was the overall technical management of the rocket. This started by ensuring an effective breakdown of work by subteam. Following this, I worked closely with all subteams, ensuring that a proper design approach was followed including requirements analysis, preliminary & critical design reviews, component manufacturing and testing, and integration testing.

I also worked as needed on many of the projects that made up Cypress. I analysed the mission and trajectory, derived core rocket specs such as motor thrust, dimensions and mass, did carbon fibre layups using vacuum bagging, extensively used and troubleshot various 3D printers, learned detailed 3D modeling using OnShape, manufactured a significant portion of rocket internal components using the waterjet, lathes, mills and drill presses (several hundreds of hours), designed and built circuitry for recovery system testing using a Teensy microcontroller, procured the large majority of UBC Rocket's components and raw materials and much more. More info can be seen below in A Closer Look.

Besides the technical work, I was also heavily involved in the admin side of the team. Being a new team, vision and direction was very important. Other team members looked to me for direction and motivation and I put significant effort into making sure that every work session was well organized and prepared. In addition, early in the team's existence, I heavily focused on raising the necessary funds for the team's success. This led me to raise the majority of our ~$30,000 budget. In addition, I was the point of contact for any issues that came up, including technical challenges, management challenges, and interpersonal issues. This allowed me to grow immensely on a professional level and further taught me what works and what doesn't work in a leadership role.

What's next

We are just starting out on this year's projects. We had immense interest in UBC Rocket and grew to a team of 100, now the largest Engineering Design Team at UBC. Luckily we have many, amazing returning members who are taking on leadership roles, so that we can focus on several ambitious projects. These include upgrading Cypress for rapid relaunch, building a new 10k (10,000ft apogee) rocket, a 30k rocket and also starting R&D towards our space rocket. The R&D team is building a high altitude demonstrator rocket and working on a liquid-propellant rocket engine.
